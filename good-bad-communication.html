Good vs Bad Communication Examples

Learn what works and what doesn't in real disaster call scenarios

Scenario 1: Panicked Caller About Evacuation

A caller is frightened and confused about whether they need to evacuate

❌

Bad Example

Caller:
"I'm so scared! Do I need to leave my house right now? I don't know what to do!"
Volunteer:
"Calm down. What's your address?"
Caller:
"I'm at 123 Oak Street, but—"
Volunteer:
"Okay, hold on, let me check... Looks like you're fine. No evacuation order for your area."

Why This Doesn't Work:

  • Dismissive tone with "calm down"
  • No empathy for caller's fear
  • Abrupt, cold responses
  • Doesn't validate emotions
  • No reassurance or guidance
✅

Good Example

Caller:
"I'm so scared! Do I need to leave my house right now? I don't know what to do!"
Volunteer:
"I understand you're frightened, and I'm here to help you. Let me check the official evacuation information for your address. Can you tell me where you're located?"
Caller:
"I'm at 123 Oak Street."
Volunteer:
"Thank you. I'm checking our disaster portal now... Good news—there's currently no evacuation order for your area. You're safe to stay home. I can also give you information about Alert San Diego so you'll get immediate notifications if that changes."

Why This Works:

  • Validates emotions immediately
  • Warm, reassuring tone
  • Explains what you're doing
  • Provides clear, actionable information
  • Offers additional resources

Scenario 2: Caller Sharing Unverified Information

A caller heard something on social media and wants confirmation

❌

Bad Example

Caller:
"I saw on Facebook that Highway 5 is closed. Is that true?"
Volunteer:
"Yeah, I heard that too. It's probably closed."
Caller:
"Are you sure? I need to get to work."
Volunteer:
"I mean, that's what people are saying. You should probably find another route just to be safe."

Why This Doesn't Work:

  • Relies on rumors ("I heard that too")
  • Doesn't check official sources
  • Uncertain language ("probably," "I mean")
  • No verified information provided
  • Violates the golden rule
✅

Good Example

Caller:
"I saw on Facebook that Highway 5 is closed. Is that true?"
Volunteer:
"Let me check our official disaster portal for the most current road closure information. One moment please."
Caller:
"Okay, thank you."
Volunteer:
"According to our disaster portal, Highway 5 North is currently closed between exits 23 and 27. The southbound lanes remain open. Would you like me to suggest alternate routes for your commute?"

Why This Works:

  • Uses only verified information
  • Checks disaster portal, not social media
  • Confident, specific information
  • Follows the golden rule
  • Offers additional assistance

Scenario 3: Angry Caller Who's Been Waiting

A caller is frustrated after waiting on hold during a crisis

❌

Bad Example

Caller:
"I've been on hold for 20 minutes! This is ridiculous! I need help NOW!"
Volunteer:
"Well, we're very busy right now. Everyone is calling. What do you need?"
Caller:
"You don't understand, my elderly mother—"
Volunteer:
"Sir, I need you to calm down so I can help you."

Why This Doesn't Work:

  • Defensive and dismissive tone
  • Makes excuses instead of apologizing
  • Interrupts the caller
  • Commands caller to "calm down"
  • Doesn't acknowledge frustration
✅

Good Example

Caller:
"I've been on hold for 20 minutes! This is ridiculous! I need help NOW!"
Volunteer:
"I'm so sorry you've had to wait, and I understand your frustration. You have my full attention now. What's happening that you need help with?"
Caller:
"My elderly mother needs special transportation to evacuate, and I don't know how to get her help."
Volunteer:
"I'm glad you called. Let me connect you with the resources for vulnerable populations who need evacuation assistance. What's your mother's location?"

Why This Works:

  • Sincere apology for the wait
  • Validates their frustration
  • Assures them they have your focus
  • Lets them fully explain the situation
  • Takes immediate action to help

Scenario 4: Caller Using Complex Technical Terms

A caller is asking about technical aspects you don't fully understand

❌

Bad Example

Caller:
"What's the air quality index in the affected zone? Is it safe for people with COPD?"
Volunteer:
"Um, I'm not sure about that. I think it's probably fine? I don't really know about COPD stuff."
Caller:
"This is important—my wife has breathing problems."
Volunteer:
"Maybe call your doctor about that? I just have information about shelters."

Why This Doesn't Work:

  • Admits uncertainty with "I think probably"
  • Dismisses importance of question
  • Passes the buck instead of helping
  • Doesn't check available resources
  • Misses chance to escalate appropriately
✅

Good Example

Caller:
"What's the air quality index in the affected zone? Is it safe for people with COPD?"
Volunteer:
"That's an important question, especially for someone with respiratory concerns. Let me check our disaster portal for current air quality information in your area. What's your zip code?"
Caller:
"92037. My wife has COPD and I'm worried about the smoke."
Volunteer:
"I completely understand your concern. Our portal shows the air quality is currently unhealthy for sensitive groups in that area. I'd recommend she stay indoors with windows closed. I can also connect you with our health resources line for specific medical guidance about COPD during this event."

Why This Works:

  • Acknowledges importance of question
  • Shows empathy for health concern
  • Checks official information source
  • Provides clear, actionable guidance
  • Offers to escalate for medical specifics

Scenario 5: Caller Asking When They Can Return Home

An evacuated caller desperately wants to know when they can go home

❌

Bad Example

Caller:
"When can I go back to my house? I need to get my medications and feed my cat. Please, I just want to go home."
Volunteer:
"I don't know. They haven't said. You just have to wait."
Caller:
"But everything I own is there. Don't you have any information?"
Volunteer:
"No. Call back later."

Why This Doesn't Work:

  • Blunt, uncaring responses
  • No empathy for emotional situation
  • Doesn't explain why information is unavailable
  • Misses concerns about medication and pet
  • Provides no alternative help or resources
✅

Good Example

Caller:
"When can I go back to my house? I need to get my medications and feed my cat. Please, I just want to go home."
Volunteer:
"I hear how difficult this is for you, and I wish I could give you an exact time. Right now, the evacuation order is still in place while emergency crews assess safety. What I can do is help you with your immediate concerns about medication and your cat."
Caller:
"Really? What can you do?"
Volunteer:
"For your medications, I can connect you with emergency prescription assistance. For your cat, we have animal welfare teams who can do wellness checks on pets in evacuated areas. I'll also make sure you're signed up for Alert San Diego so you get immediate notification the moment your area is cleared for return."

Why This Works:

  • Deep empathy and validation
  • Honest about limitations
  • Explains why timing is uncertain
  • Addresses each specific concern
  • Provides actionable solutions and hope
💡

The Pattern You Should See

Good communication combines empathy, clarity, verified information, and action. It's not about having all the answers—it's about showing you care, using official sources, staying calm, and helping callers find solutions. Your tone, words, and approach can transform someone's worst day into a moment where they feel heard, helped, and hopeful.
